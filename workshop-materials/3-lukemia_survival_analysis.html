It refers to the degree to which the disease has responded to treatment, particularly whether cancerous cells can still be detected in the body. time to event (in days) is the duration until remission or censoring. logWBC (Log of white blood cell count) serves as a key clinical biomarker. rx represents treatment protocol, indicates the specific therapeutic approach implemented for each patient. This dataset structure enables multifaceted analysis of factors influencing leukemia remission, allowing researchers to identify both clinical and demographic predictors of patient outcomes.

Kaplan-Meier analysis

Kaplan-Meier analysis provides a non-parametric approach to estimating survival functions, offering a robust initial assessment of remission patterns before proceeding to more complex regression models. However, unlike models such as the Cox proportional hazards model, Kaplan–Meier is not used to predict individual survival. Instead, it estimates group-level survival probabilities at observed time points. Calculating the survival probability at each time point using the formula: \[ S(t) = \prod_{i: t_i \le t} \left(1 - \frac{d_i}{n_i} \right) \] Where:

  • \(S(t)\): Survival probability at time \(t\)
  • \(t_i\): Time of the \(i\)-th event (e.g., death, relapse)
  • \(d_i\): Number of events (e.g., deaths) at time \(t_i\)
  • \(n_i\): Number of individuals at risk just before time \(t_i\)

This step-function estimation accounts for both events and censored observations.

Cox proportional hazards regression

Cox regression enables simultaneous assessment of multiple variables’ impact on leukemia remission. For our dataset, this means quantifying how sex, logWBC, and treatment protocol collectively influence hazard rates while controlling for confounding factors. This semi-parametric approach offers flexibility without requiring specification of the baseline hazard function, making it ideal for identifying significant predictors of leukemia remission.

The Cox proportional hazards model specifies the hazard function as

\[ h(t) = h_0(t) \times \exp(\beta_1 X_1 + \beta_2 X_2 + \cdots + \beta_p X_p), \]

where \(h_0(t)\) is the baseline hazard and \(X_1\), \(X_2\), , \(X_p\) are the covariates. Coefficient estimation is performed using partial likelihood, which allows for the computation of hazard ratios associated with each predictor. To ensure the model’s validity, the proportional hazards assumption is evaluated using Schoenfeld residuals. Model diagnostics, such as martingale residuals and influence measures, are employed to assess the adequacy of the model fit and identify potential outliers or influential observations.

Model validation - I

There are various ways to measure model performance. Concordance Index (C-index) is a summary metric of predictive discrimination. The values ranges from 0.5 (random) to 1.0 (perfect prediction). 0.7 is generally considered acceptable. We observe a C-index of 0.8513324, 0.0405009, indicating that the model has good predictive ability.

Conclusion & next steps

In this analysis, we explored the impact of various factors on leukemia remission using survival analysis techniques. The Kaplan-Meier curve provided a visual representation of survival probabilities, while the Cox proportional hazards model allowed us to quantify the influence of multiple predictors on remission outcomes. Following are the key take aways:

  • White blood cell count serves as a critical biomarker for risk stratification, enabling more personalized treatment approaches based on initial logWBC values.
  • Experimental protocol A demonstrates superior long-term outcomes, particularly for female patients and those with lower initial WBC counts.
  • Treatment efficacy changes over time, necessitating extended follow-up periods and time-dependent analytical approaches for accurate assessment.

Future research should focus on validating these findings in larger cohorts and exploring additional biomarkers that may further refine predictive models. The methodological framework presented here provides a robust template for conducting similar analyses across other oncological contexts.
